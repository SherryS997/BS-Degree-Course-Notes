# Non-Linear Filters: Bilateral Filtering

- Noise removal comes at expense of image blurring at edges
- **Bilateral filtering**: Simple, non-linear edge-preserving smoothing
  - Reject (in a soft manner) pixels whose values differ too much from the central pixel value.
  - Output pixel value is weighted combination of neighboring pixel values: 
    \[
    g(i, j) = \frac{\sum_{k, l} I(k, l) w(i, j, k, l)}{\sum_{k, l} w(i, j, k, l)}
    \]
  - Data-dependent bilateral weight function composed of domain and range kernel:
    \[
    w(i, j, k, l) = \exp \left( - \frac{(i - k)^2 + (j - l)^2}{2\sigma_d^2} - \frac{\|I(i, j) - I(k, l)\|^2}{2\sigma_r^2} \right)
    \]

## The Fourier Spectrum

### Introduction
The Fourier spectrum is a fundamental concept in signal processing and physics, which describes the frequency content of a signal. It is named after the French mathematician Jean-Baptiste Joseph Fourier, who developed the mathematical theory behind it.

### Mathematical Description
The Fourier spectrum of a signal \(x(t)\) is obtained by taking the Fourier transform of the signal. The Fourier transform \(X(f)\) is defined as:

\[ X(f) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t) e^{-j2\pi f t} \, dt \]

where:
- \(x(t)\) is the time-domain signal.
- \(X(f)\) is the frequency-domain representation.
- \(f\) is the frequency variable.
- \(j\) is the imaginary unit.

### Properties of the Fourier Transform
1. **Linearity**: The Fourier transform is a linear operation. If \(x(t)\) and \(y(t)\) are two signals, then:

\[ \mathcal{F}\{ax(t) + by(t)\} = aX(f) + bY(f) \]

2. **Convolution**: The convolution of two time-domain signals \(x(t)\) and \(y(t)\) in the time domain corresponds to multiplication in the frequency domain:

\[ \mathcal{F}\{x(t) * y(t)\} = X(f)Y(f) \]

3. **Shifting**: A time shift in the time domain corresponds to a phase shift in the frequency domain:

\[ \mathcal{F}\{x(t - a)\} = X(f) e^{-j2\pi f a} \]

4. **Scaling**: A time scaling in the time domain corresponds to a frequency scaling in the frequency domain:

\[ \mathcal{F}\{x(at)\} = \frac{1}{|a|} X\left(\frac{f}{a}\right) \]

### Practical Applications
The Fourier spectrum is widely used in various fields, including:
- **Signal Processing**: For analyzing and filtering signals.
- **Image Processing**: For edge detection and noise reduction.
- **Physics**: For analyzing wave phenomena and quantum mechanics.
- **Communication Systems**: For modulation and demodulation.

### Example
Consider a rectangular pulse in the time domain:

\[ x(t) = \begin{cases}
1 & \text{if } 0 \leq t \leq 1 \\
0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases} \]

The Fourier transform of this signal is:

\[ X(f) = \frac{\sin(\pi f)}{\pi f} \]

This is known as the **sinc function**.

### Conclusion
The Fourier spectrum provides a powerful tool for understanding the frequency content of signals, enabling more efficient signal processing and analysis in various applications.

## Diagram Explanation

### Top Row

1. **Green Square Wave**
   - The green square wave on the left represents a periodic discontinuous signal.
   - This signal is shown as a series of sharp transitions between high and low values.

2. **Fourier Series Components**
   - The square wave can be decomposed into a series of sine waves.
   - The upper middle part shows the combination of several sine waves that sum up to recreate the square wave.

3. **Resultant Wave**
   - The resultant wave on the right exhibits a continuous periodic wave.
   - This is the Fourier series representation of the original square wave.

### Bottom Row

1. **Alternate Fourier Series Components**
   - Similar to the top row, this section shows a different combination of sine waves.
   - These waves are also summed to produce the same square wave.

2. **Resultant Wave**
   - Again, the resultant continuous wave on the right is the Fourier series representation of the square wave.

## Mathematical Representation

The Fourier series representation of a periodic signal \( f(t) \) can be expressed as:
\[ f(t) = a_0 + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left( a_n \cos(n\omega t) + b_n \sin(n\omega t) \right) \]

Where:
- \( a_0 \) is the zero-frequency component (dc term).
- \( a_n \) and \( b_n \) are the Fourier coefficients.
- \( \omega \) is the angular frequency.

For a square wave:
- \( a_0 = 0 \)
- The coefficients \( a_n \) and \( b_n \) are determined by the specific properties of the square wave.

### Example of Fourier Coefficients Calculation

For a square wave with amplitude \( A \) and period \( T \):
\[ a_n = \frac{2A}{n\pi} \text{ for even } n \]
\[ b_n = 0 \text{ for all } n \]

This represents how the square wave can be constructed from a combination of sine waves of varying frequencies and amplitudes.

# Fourier Analysis of Images

- Encodes a whole series of sinusoids through a range of spatial frequencies from zero all the way up to ‘Nyquist frequency’ (more on this later)

- Signal containing only a single spatial frequency of frequency \( f \) is plotted as:
  - a single peak at point \( f \) along spatial frequency axis

# Continuous vs Discrete Fourier Transform

![Fourier Transform Image](link-to-image)

## Continuous Fourier transform (FT):

- **Formula:**
  \[
  H(\omega) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} h(x) e^{-j \omega x} dx
  \]

## Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT):

- **Formula:**
  \[
  H(\omega) = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} h(x) e^{-j \frac{2 \pi n x}{N}} dx
  \]
  where \(N\) is the length of the sampled signal.

## Process:

1. **Fourier Transform:**
   - **Signal:**
     - Input Signal
   - **Magnitude Spectrum:**
     - Output as a Continuous Spectrum

2. **Copy and Shift:**
   - **Process:**
     - Copy and shift the signal to process further

3. **Fourier Transform:**
   - **Sampled Signal:**
     - Input as sampled signal
   - **Magnitude Spectrum:**
     - Output as Discrete Spectrum

## Diagram:

- **Magnitude Spectrum:**
  - Shows the output of the Fourier Transform representing frequency components.

# Convolution Theorem

- **Fourier transform of convolution of two functions is product of their Fourier transforms:**

  \[
  F[g * h] = F[g]F[h]
  \]

- **Convolution in spatial domain can be obtained through multiplication in frequency domain!**

  \[
  g * h = F^{-1}[F[g]F[h]]
  \]

# Properties of Fourier Transform

| Property             | Signal                                      | Transform                          |
|----------------------|---------------------------------------------|------------------------------------|
| superposition        | \( f_1(x) + f_2(x) \)                       | \( F_1(\omega) + F_2(\omega) \)    |
| shift                | \( f(x - x_0) \)                            | \( F(\omega)e^{-j\omega x_0} \)    |
| reversal             | \( f(-x) \)                                 | \( F^*(\omega) \)                   |
| convolution          | \( f(x) * h(x) \)                           | \( F(\omega)H(\omega) \)           |
| correlation          | \( f(x) \otimes h(x) \)                     | \( F(\omega)H^*(\omega) \)          |
| multiplication       | \( f(x)h(x) \)                              | \( F(\omega) * H(\omega) \)         |
| differentiation      | \( f'(x) \)                                 | \( j\omega F(\omega) \)             |
| domain scaling       | \( f(ax) \)                                 | \( 1/a F(\omega/a) \)               |
| real images          | \( f(x) = f^*(x) \)                         | \( F(\omega) = F(-\omega) \)        |
| Parseval's Theorem   | \( \sum_x |f(x)|^2 \)                          | \( \sum_\omega |F(\omega)|^2 \)          |

*Credit: Szeliski, Computer Vision: Algorithms and Applications, 2010*

## Process Overview

1. The filter matrix is applied to each pixel of the input intensity image.
2. The center pixel is multiplied by the central element of the filter matrix.
3. The surrounding pixels are multiplied by the corresponding filter elements.
4. The results of these multiplications are summed to produce a single output pixel value.

# Filtering in Frequency Domain

## Diagram Explanation

This diagram illustrates the process of filtering in the frequency domain. The steps are as follows:

1. **Input Image**: The process starts with an input image. 
   ![Input Image](image-url)

2. **FFT (Fast Fourier Transform)**: The input image undergoes a Fast Fourier Transform (FFT). This converts the image from the spatial domain to the frequency domain, which highlights different frequency components.
   ![FFT Result](fft-image-url)

3. **Filtering**: In the frequency domain, specific frequency components are filtered. The filtering operation is represented by multiplying the FFT result with a filter (shown as a red and blue matrix).
   ![Filtering](filtering-image-url)

4. **Inverse FFT**: The filtered frequency domain data is then processed with an Inverse Fast Fourier Transform (Inverse FFT) to convert it back to the spatial domain.
   ![Inverse FFT Result](inverse-fft-image-url)

5. **Output**: The final output image is produced, which has been processed through the frequency domain filtering.

## Image Breakdown

### Intensity Image
- **Description**: This is the original input image.
- **Credit**: Derek Hoeim, UIUC

### FFT Result
- **Description**: This is the frequency domain representation of the input image.
- **Symbol**: FFT

### Filtering
- **Description**: In this step, specific frequency components are filtered.
- **Symbol**: × (Multiplication)

### Inverse FFT Result
- **Description**: This is the result after applying the Inverse FFT to the filtered frequency domain data.
- **Symbol**: Inverse FFT
